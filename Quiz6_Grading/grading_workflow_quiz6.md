## Module 1 — Input & Setup

### Case Study Summary
Students were given the OMDC scenario: a new PM inherits an MS Office installation project with four team members:

| Member | Profile | Key motivational context |
|---|---|---|
| **Zichen** | Lead MS Office technician; experienced; bored; wants to move into management | Needs growth opportunity; management track motivation |
| **Natasha** | Newest hire; CS degree; eager; lacks MS Office experience; must prove herself | Needs learning opportunity; mentorship; confidence-building |
| **Ramesh** | Experienced network admin; certified; limited MS Office dev interest; worried about job security | Needs reassurance; keep in comfort zone (network tasks); protect job security concern |
| **Amando** | Deployment expert; suspicious of change; prefers academic-researcher work; pursuing master's degree | Needs transparency; trust; autonomy; connection to meaningful work |

### Quiz Questions
- **Q1 (2 marks):** What methods might you use to motivate each of the team members on this project?
- **Q2 (2 marks):** List leadership qualities that are necessary to ensure the team works well together.

## Module 3 — Rubric: Q1 (Motivation Methods, 2 marks)

### What must be evaluated

The professor's key instruction: **students are tested on application of course material, not common sense.** An answer that is merely sensible but does not reference lecture concepts (leadership styles, Cohen-Bradford currencies, Keys to Leadership, Drucker/social intelligence) scores lower than one that applies course vocabulary.

Q1 asks students to address **each of the four team members**. Coverage and grounding in course material are both required.

### Lecture material that should appear in strong Q1 answers

**Leadership Styles (applied per member):**
- Visionary, Coaching, Affiliative, Democratic, Pacesetting, Commanding

**Cohen-Bradford Influence Currencies (5 types):**
- Inspiration-Related: appeal to values, cause, integrity
- Task-Related: offer expertise, material goods, skill exchange
- Position-Related: recognition, reputation, visibility, public acknowledgement
- Relationship-Related: networking, bonding, team membership, affiliation
- Personal-Related: freedom, autonomy, doing what one likes

**Keys to Leadership (8 points):**
People-first, See with their eyes, Earn trust, Earn respect, Take ownership, Compensate for weaknesses, Lead by example, Understand what motivates

**Professor-noted example answer:** Assign Zichen to oversee Natasha's work — covers her inexperience, gives him management experience. This is acceptable even though it's "common sense" because it solves two problems at once.

### Per-member motivation indicators (what to look for)

| Member | Strong motivational approaches | Weak / common sense only |
|---|---|---|
| Zichen | Position-related currency (visibility, promotion path); coaching leadership; assign management responsibility over Natasha | "Give him a challenge" / "acknowledge good work" — no course connection |
| Natasha | Coaching leadership; task-related currency (skill development); pair with Zichen for mentorship; frame contribution as meaningful | "Be patient" / "give her time" — no course connection |
| Ramesh | Affiliative leadership; personal-related currency (autonomy, stay in comfort zone); reassure job security; limit MS Office dev exposure | "Talk to him" / "reassure him" — without connecting to any model |
| Amando | Inspiration-related currency (appeal to values/significance); relationship-related currency (team connection); transparency about changes; tie project to academic mission | "Tell him it's important" — without naming a currency or style |

### Q1 Holistic Scoring Scale

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **2.0 / 2.0** | All 4 members addressed. Each has at least one motivation strategy that clearly connects to a course concept (named currency, leadership style, or key). Strategies are specific to that member's situation. |
| **1.5 / 2.0** | 3–4 members addressed with course references, OR all 4 addressed but 1–2 have only surface-level or common-sense strategies with no lecture connection. |
| **1.0 / 2.0** | 2–3 members addressed adequately with some course grounding, OR all 4 addressed but primarily common-sense language throughout with little or no course terminology. |
| **0.5 / 2.0** | Only 1–2 members addressed, or answer is very brief and generic with no course grounding. |
| **0 / 2.0** | Not answered, blank, or completely off-topic. |

**Edge case — "only 3 members addressed":** Default to 1.5 if the 3 addressed are well-grounded in course material. If quality is also low, use 1.0.

## Module 4 — Rubric: Q2 (Leadership Qualities, 2 marks)

### What must be evaluated

Professor's instruction: **must reference course material, not just common sense.** Students who list "be a good communicator" or "be organized" without tying to any lecture concept score lower.

At least **2 qualities** from lecture content = full marks threshold.

### Lecture material that should appear in strong Q2 answers

Any of the following count as course-sourced:
- Named leadership style (Visionary, Coaching, Affiliative, Democratic, Pacesetting, Commanding)
- Any of the 8 Keys to Leadership (by name or clear paraphrase: earn trust, earn respect, lead by example, take ownership, compensate for weaknesses, see with their eyes, understand what motivates, people-first)
- Cohen-Bradford currencies applied to leadership
- Drucker Exercise / social intelligence concept

### Q2 Scoring Scale

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **2.0 / 2.0** | 2 or more qualities named that clearly come from lecture material (styles, keys, or currencies). May include brief case connection or explanation. |
| **1.5 / 2.0** | 2+ qualities listed, mostly from lecture, but one is clearly only common sense. OR 1 strong lecture-sourced quality with good case application. |
| **1.0 / 2.0** | 1 lecture-sourced quality clearly identifiable, rest is common sense. OR 2+ qualities listed but only recognizable as lecture content through loose paraphrase. |
| **0.5 / 2.0** | All qualities are common sense with no discernible course connection. Shows minimal engagement. |
| **0 / 2.0** | Not answered, blank, or completely off-topic. |

## Module 5 — AI Detection Indicators

Flag for TA review (grade normally, do not withhold) when the submission shows:
- Formal academic citation style (e.g., "Cohen and Bradford (2005)" with year)
- Complete structured lists that mirror lecture slides almost verbatim
- Covering every course concept in the correct order
- Unusual precision ("five currencies" enumerated in correct order with definitions)
- Language noticeably more formal or structured than the surrounding class submissions

Use `ai_flag = Y` and populate `ai_flag_reason` with 1–2 specific observations.

---

## Module 6 — Human Review Triggers

Flag `human_review = Y` when:
- Score sits at a borderline (e.g., 1.5 vs 2.0 is a judgment call)
- Answer addresses the question but in an unexpected or creative way not covered by rubric
- Common-sense answer is very insightful — may warrant more credit than rubric default
- Missing name or student number
- Very low score (below 1.5 total) — double-check before finalizing
- Late submission with unclear timestamp
- Submission format is incorrect or partially unreadable

---

## Module 7 — Score Aggregation

```
raw_score = q1_score + q2_score

late_deduction = raw_score × late_penalty_pct

final_score = raw_score - late_deduction
             (rounded to 2 decimal places, minimum 0)
```

## Module 9 — Consistency Checks

Before finalizing the CSV, verify:

1. **Q1 full-credit check:** Any student scoring 2.0 on Q1 must address all 4 members with at least one course-grounded strategy per member. Spot-check 3–4 randomly.
2. **Q2 course-grounding check:** Any 2.0 on Q2 must have 2+ identifiable lecture concepts. Spot-check borderline 1.5 vs 2.0 cases.
3. **Common-sense trap:** If a student says "Zichen needs a challenge" without naming a currency or style, that is NOT full credit on Q1 for Zichen.
4. **Score drift:** If grading more than 20 submissions, pause and re-read the first 3 graded vs the current batch to check for drift.
5. **Late penalty arithmetic:** Verify that `raw_score - late_deduction = final_score` for all LATE rows.
6. **Student number format:** Flag any non-10-digit IDs for Quercus verification.

1. Receive and extract submissions (.doc, .docx, .rtf) to text

5. Check for AI indicators (Module 5)
6. Flag human review cases (Module 6)
7. Sum raw_score
8. Apply late penalty if timestamp known; otherwise mark CHECK_QUERCUS
9. Generate quercus_comment (Module 10)
10. Write CSV (Module 8)
11. Run consistency checks (Module 9)
12. Generate grading summary report
